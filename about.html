What's the goal of The Pinball Primer?

The Pinball Primer is designed to be a one-stop shop for anyone looking to learn a pinball table of any era. Whether you're playing casually or in a tournament, and whether you're new to pinball or have years of experience, my vision is for The Pinball Primer to be a place where you can spend 30 seconds to quickly learn the strategy of any table you walk up to, or spend about 5 minutes deeply learning all the rules to truly understand how the game works.

Why are you doing this? Doesn't [other resource] exist?

Yes, other resources do exist, but spottiness in their coverage or quality is precisely why I want to make The Pinball Primer a thorough, go-to resource. Inspiration was taken from some of the following places:

  • The Bob Matthews EMcyclopedia. Bob Matthews is one of the longest tenured and most knowledgeable pinball players of all time, with a competition career spanning many decades. He literally wrote the book on strategies for electromechanical tables- that's what is linked here, in wiki form. His "Quickie Version -> Full Detail" page setup directly inspired the layout of my pages. Unfortunately, his wiki doesn't cover any tables released after about 1985, and on many tables, some strategies or scoring features that aren't necessarily competition-viable are omitted or have their details incorrect.
  • The PAPA/Replay Foundation rule sheets. The Professional & Amateur Pinball Association hosts rulesheets for dozens and dozens of tables, primarily alphanumeric and 1990s DMD games, written by the best players in the world when those tables were new. Since they were written when the tables were new, though, many of them are 20-25 years old or older, and may have incomplete or incorrect rules, or outdated strategies.
  • Pintips. Pintips is a user submitted forum for super quick notes and how-tos in pinball, but in my experience, the quality is inconsistent at best, as the 1-2 sentence posts can be unclear in what they're referring to or exclude discussion on tables that have multiple ways to play.

The Pinball Primer was not created to plagiarize the hard work of any of these people; it is merely intended to compile, refine, and expand upon it to help lower the barrier of entry for anyone who wants to pick up, learn, or just generally have a good time playing the game of pinball.

Why are so many of the website's pages about games from the late 70s and early 80s? / Why does it seem like certain eras and manufacturers are omitted?

My (solar_espeon's) pinball interest lies the most in late electromechanical and early solid-state games. For me personally, they're the most fun and the easiest to learn, and they also take less time to write a detailed page on. Information on newer games is coming, but as this website is still relatively new, I'm currently sticking with what I know best. Diversity of covered games will improve as the website continues to grow and expand. Also, for some specific manufacturers, there are additional barriers to the site authors being able to master a game's rules:

  • Jersey Jack actively seeks to prevent virtual pinball communities from making any kind of recreations of their games, even those that are no longer on sale. This means that capacity to learn these games is much more limited by the ability of the site's authors to find and play a physical copy of the table, which is much more time- and cost-intensive.
  • Stern Pinball's LCD games (2016-present) present some similar issues as Jersey Jack due to being too new to have virtual recreations. Also, all of Stern Pinball's LCD games released after Batman 66 (2016) have both abridged and completed rulesets in their Insider Connected app. These two points combined mean that, while one should never say never, Stern's newest games have very low priority for receiving pages on this site. These concerns and barriers do not apply to the majority of Stern Pinball's DMD games (1999-2015), of which a few games already have tutorials on The Pinball Primer with more to come.
  • The solid state games from Gottlieb/Mylstar/Premier (1978-96) can be rather difficult to have their rules details verified. The people who hold the rights to the Gottlieb company name are inexplicably much more strict about copyrighted materials relating to their games than other defunct companies, and they are very quick to strike down internet distributions of schematics, parts lists, ROMs, and manuals that they are not able to make money off of, even though the company has been out of business for nearly thirty years. This means that writing a guide for any Gottlieb brand table with a complex ruleset that meets my own quality expectations would take significantly more time to produce. Add in the fact that public perception of Gottlieb games from these eras is that they are far inferior to their contemporaries from other companies, and there just ends up being very little demand for these guides or desire to write them outside of direct requests.

As a disclaimer: none of the material included on any page within the Pinball Primer website is intended to infringe on any copyright. All copyrighted terms, names of licensed games, etc., belongs to their respective owners. The information disseminated on this website is for educational purposes only.

Why do multiple links on the Game List page go to the same guide page?

Pinball games get re-released for a myriad of reasons, such as having both solid state and electromechanical versions, having versions with different numbers of players, trying to recapitalize on the success of a previously successful layout, or updating a licensed game in time with the release of other related media. If a table was released multiple times under different names with minimal rules changes, they appear under all different names on the game list and are counted separately to help a player that may not know that a game they're looking at has a "sister title", but the different links lead to the same page for efficiency's sake. Such guide pages that detail a table with multiple releases will include notes on the different versions of that game at the top of the page.

Can I request that a specific table be added?

Sure! Just don't expect it to be ready immediately. The Pinball Primer is a labor of love project that Sammie and I work on in our free time, and it takes a fair amount of that time to learn a table and then do the write-up on it. Feel free to reach out, though, and we'll do what we can.
